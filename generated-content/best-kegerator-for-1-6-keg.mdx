## What to Look for Before Buying the Best Kegerator For 1 6 Keg

### Find Your Need - Compatibility and use case considerations

When choosing a kegerator for a 1/6 keg, first consider your specific use case. Determine whether you'll primarily serve standard 1/6 barrel commercial kegs or Cornelius homebrew kegs, as compatibility varies. Think about where you'll place the unit—indoors, outdoors, or both—and whether you need features like weather resistance or a reversible door. Consider how many taps you require: a single tap suffices for one beer, while multiple taps let you offer variety. Also evaluate your expected usage: occasional gatherings may warrant a more affordable model, while frequent entertaining benefits from a robust, full-size unit. Matching the kegerator to your needs ensures long-term satisfaction and value.

### Budget - Setting realistic price expectations ($X-$Y ranges)

Kegerator prices span a wide range depending on size, features, and build quality. For a basic conversion kit that adds a tap to an existing fridge, expect to spend $100-$300. Compact 1/6 keg-specific models like the EdgeStar fall between $300-$600. Full-size single or dual tap kegerators typically run $600-$1,200. Outdoor or multi-functional units with extra features can exceed $1,500. Set a budget that balances your desired features with long-term durability. Remember that additional costs—CO2 tanks, extra kegs, and cleaning supplies—should be factored in. Investing in a reputable brand often pays off with better reliability and warranty support.

### Key Features - Most important technical specifications

Key technical features to prioritize include temperature range and control. A range of 35-42°F suits most beers, while some models go as low as 32°F for lagers. Digital thermostats offer precise settings, while mechanical ones are simpler. Cooling method matters: fan-forced systems circulate air for even temperatures, while conventional cooling may have hot spots. Look for energy-efficient designs to save on electricity. Ensure the unit includes essential accessories—CO2 tank, regulator, keg coupler(s), and draft tower—to avoid extra purchases. Also consider capacity: can it fit your desired keg sizes and possibly additional shelves for storage? Auto-defrost and deep chill functions add convenience for heavy use.

### Quality & Durability - What to look for in construction

Durability hinges on materials and construction. Stainless steel interiors and exteriors resist corrosion and are easy to clean. A stainless steel floor plate protects the cabinet from weight damage and allows smooth keg movement. Look for CSA or UL listings, indicating safety and quality. The compressor should be robust, ideally with a multi-year warranty. Quality seals and gaskets prevent air leaks and maintain temperature. Casters, if included, should lock securely. Reversible doors offer installation flexibility. Read reviews to gauge long-term reliability. A well-built kegerator will last 5-10 years with proper care, making it a worthwhile investment.

### Brand Reputation - Importance of established manufacturers

Choosing a reputable brand ensures access to reliable customer support, warranty service, and quality-assured components. Established manufacturers like Kegco, EdgeStar, HCK, and MRbrew have years of experience and positive feedback from the home beverage community. They typically use UL-recognized parts and adhere to safety standards. Research each brand's warranty policies—most offer 1-3 years on parts and compressors. A strong brand presence also means easier access to replacement parts and troubleshooting resources. While lesser-known brands may be cheaper, they often lack the track record for longevity. Opting for a trusted name provides peace of mind and a better ownership experience.

### Portability/Size - Considerations for space and storage

The physical dimensions and weight of a kegerator are crucial, especially for small living spaces. Measure your intended location, including height, width, and depth, and allow clearance for ventilation. Compact models like the EdgeStar (17.5" wide) fit under counters, while full-size units require more floor space. If you plan to move the unit frequently—e.g., for outdoor events—look for lockable casters. Freestanding models are easier to place anywhere, while built-in units require cabinet modifications. Also consider door swing and whether a reversible door is needed. Portability features such as a carry handle (as on portable units) enhance flexibility. Ensure the unit's weight is manageable for your situation.
